Circle of Fifths

The Ultimate Music Theory Map

What Is the Circle of Fifths?

The Circle of Fifths is a visual diagram that shows the relationship between all 12 keys in music. It's one of the most powerful tools in music theory because it reveals patterns that help with:

  • Key signatures: How many sharps or flats each key has
  • Chord progressions: Which chords sound good together
  • Modulation: Changing keys smoothly
  • Relative keys: Finding the minor key of a major key (and vice versa)
  • Composition: Building interesting harmonic progressions

How It's Organized

The circle arranges all 12 major keys in a clockwise pattern, with each key being a perfect fifth (7 semitones) higher than the previous one:

The Circle (Clockwise)
C → G → D → A → E → B → F#/Gb → Db → Ab → Eb → Bb → F → C
Each step moves up by a perfect fifth (7 semitones)

Visual Layout

Imagine a clock face where each hour represents a key:

C (12)
F (11)
G (1)
Bb (10)
🔄
D (2)
Eb (9)
A (3)
Ab (8)
E (4)
Db (7)
B (5)
F#/Gb (6)
Numbers show "clock position" - C is at the top (12 o'clock)

Sharps and Flats Pattern

One of the most useful features: the circle shows how many sharps or flats each key has!

Direction Pattern Keys
Clockwise (Right) Add 1 sharp each step C → G (1#) → D (2#) → A (3#) → E (4#) → B (5#)
Counter-clockwise (Left) Add 1 flat each step C → F (1♭) → Bb (2♭) → Eb (3♭) → Ab (4♭) → Db (5♭)
💡 Easy to Remember:
Sharps go clockwise (right) - think "sharp turn right"
Flats go counter-clockwise (left)

Order of Sharps and Flats

The circle also reveals the ORDER in which sharps and flats appear in key signatures:

Order of Sharps (clockwise): F# - C# - G# - D# - A# - E# - B#
Mnemonic: Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle
Order of Flats (counter-clockwise): Bb - Eb - Ab - Db - Gb - Cb - Fb
Mnemonic: Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles' Father
(Notice it's the reverse of the sharps!)

Relative Minor Keys

Every major key has a relative minor that shares the same key signature. On the circle, the relative minor is found by moving 3 steps counter-clockwise (or looking at the inner ring if the circle shows both):

Major Key Relative Minor Key Signature
C Major A Minor No sharps or flats
G Major E Minor 1 sharp (F#)
D Major B Minor 2 sharps (F#, C#)
F Major D Minor 1 flat (Bb)
Bb Major G Minor 2 flats (Bb, Eb)
🎵 Quick Trick: To find the relative minor, count down 3 semitones (or up 9 semitones) from the major key:
C → B → Bb → A (A minor is relative to C major)

Using the Circle for Chord Progressions

Keys that are close together on the circle sound good when played together. This is why the circle is so useful for composing:

Neighboring Keys (1 step apart):
Keys next to each other share many common notes and transition smoothly.

Example in C:
• C → G (neighbors) sounds natural
• C → F (neighbors) sounds natural
• C → F# (opposite side) sounds jarring
Common Progressions Using the Circle:

I - IV - V progression:
In C major: C - F - G
Notice: F is 1 step left, G is 1 step right on the circle

I - vi - IV - V progression:
In C major: C - Am - F - G
All keys are close neighbors on the circle

Modulation (Changing Keys)

When you want to change keys in a song, moving to a nearby key on the circle creates a smooth transition:

  • 1 step away: Very smooth, closely related (e.g., C to G or C to F)
  • 2 steps away: Smooth but more noticeable
  • 3 steps away: Clear key change, still musical (e.g., C to A minor - relative minor!)
  • 6 steps away (opposite side): Dramatic, distant key change

Practical Applications

1. Finding Key Signatures
"What key has 3 sharps?"
Start at C, move clockwise 3 steps: C → G → D → A major
The sharps are: F#, C#, G# (first 3 in the order of sharps)
2. Transposing Songs
"This song is in D but I want it in E"
Look at the circle: D → A → E (2 steps clockwise)
Every chord in the song moves 2 steps clockwise too!
3. Building Chord Progressions
"I'm in G major, what chords work well?"
Look at neighbors on circle: C (left), D (right), and E minor (relative minor)
Common progression: G - C - D - G (I - IV - V - I)
4. Understanding Borrowed Chords
"Why does a C major song sometimes use an Am chord?"
Am is the relative minor (3 steps counter-clockwise)
They share the same notes, so Am fits naturally in C major

How to Memorize the Circle

🎯 Memory Tips:

1. Start with C at the top (12 o'clock)
2. Move clockwise by fifths: C-G-D-A-E-B-F#
3. Remember: Each clockwise step adds one sharp
4. Or go backward: Counter-clockwise adds flats
5. Bottom (6 o'clock): The "turnaround point" where sharps meet flats (F#/Gb)
💡 Another Trick:
The circle is just the notes going up by 7 semitones repeatedly!
C (0) → G (7) → D (14=2) → A (21=9) → E (28=4) → B (35=11) → F#/Gb (42=6)
Keep adding 7 and wrap around at 12

Why It's Called "Fifths"

Each step around the circle is an interval of a perfect fifth (7 semitones or 5 scale degrees):

C to G = Perfect Fifth
C major scale: C - D - E - F - G
1st to 5th note = 5 scale degrees
Semitones: C to G = 7 semitones

This interval is special because it sounds very consonant and stable - it's the foundation of Western harmony!

🎼 Fun Fact: Going counter-clockwise is intervals of perfect fourths (5 semitones). The circle works both ways!
