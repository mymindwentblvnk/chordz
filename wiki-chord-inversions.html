Chord Inversions

Tonumkehrung / Akkordumkehrung

Once you know how to build chords, you can rearrange their notes to create different voicings. This is called a chord inversion (German: Tonumkehrung or Akkordumkehrung).

💡 The Big Idea

A chord inversion is when you take the bottom note and move it to the top (or vice versa). The chord is still the same, but it sounds different!

The Three Positions

Every triad (3-note chord) has three possible positions:

Example: C Major Chord (C + E + G)

Root Position: C - E - G
The root note (C) is on the bottom. This is the "normal" way.

First Inversion: E - G - C
Move the bottom note (C) to the top. Now E is the lowest note.

Second Inversion: G - C - E
Move the bottom note (E) to the top again. Now G is the lowest note.
Example: Am Chord (A + C + E)

Root Position: A - C - E
First Inversion: C - E - A
Second Inversion: E - A - C

Understanding Chord Note Names

Each note in a chord has a specific name based on its interval from the root:

Chord Note Terminology:

• Root (German: Grundton) - The first note, gives the chord its name
• Third (German: Terz) - The second note, determines if major or minor
• Fifth (German: Quinte) - The third note
• Bass Note (German: Basston) - The lowest note being played
Example: C Major Chord

Root Position (C - E - G):
• Root: C
• Third: E
• Fifth: G
• Bass note: C (the root is on bottom)

First Inversion (E - G - C):
• Root: C
• Third: E
• Fifth: G
• Bass note: E (the third is on bottom)

Second Inversion (G - C - E):
• Root: C
• Third: E
• Fifth: G
• Bass note: G (the fifth is on bottom)

Notice: The root, third, and fifth never change - only the bass note changes!
💡 Key Insight: The root, third, and fifth are always the same notes regardless of inversion. What changes is which note is the bass note (lowest note). This is what creates the different inversion sounds!

Why Use Inversions?

Inversions make your playing sound more professional and musical:

  1. Smoother Movement: Instead of jumping your hand all over the keyboard, you can stay in one position and just shift fingers slightly
  2. Better Bass Lines: Create more interesting bass movement by choosing which note is on the bottom
  3. Richer Sound: Different inversions have different emotional qualities - experiment to find what sounds best
  4. Voice Leading: Move between chords more smoothly by keeping common notes in place
Practical Example: I - IV - V in C Major

Without Inversions (all root position):
C (C-E-G) → F (F-A-C) → G (G-B-D)
Your hand jumps around a lot!

With Inversions (smooth voice leading):
C (C-E-G) → F (C-F-A) → G (B-D-G)
Much smoother! Notice how C stays in place, and your hand barely moves.

How to Identify Inversions

Look at which note is the bass note (lowest note):

Bass Note (Basston) Inversion Example (C chord)
Root Root Position C - E - G
Third (Terz) First Inversion E - G - C
Fifth (Quinte) Second Inversion G - C - E
🎹 Pro Tip: When playing chord progressions, try using different inversions instead of always playing root position. Your playing will sound more musical and less "choppy"! Experiment to find what sounds best to your ear.
💡 Quick Practice: Pick any chord you know (like C, G, or Am) and practice playing all three positions without stopping. This builds finger flexibility and helps you "see" the chord shapes on the keyboard.
