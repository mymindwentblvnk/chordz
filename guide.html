How to Play Chord Progressions

From Roman Numerals to Keyboard Keys

🎹 THE MASTER PATTERN

This is all you need to memorize to play in ANY key:

W - W - H - W - W - W - H
Whole - Whole - Half - Whole - Whole - Whole - Half
💡 This pattern builds a major scale from ANY starting note!
Once you know this pattern, you can play in any key. Everything else on this page just explains how to use this pattern.
Quick Definition:
• Whole step (W) = Skip one key (e.g., C → D skips C#)
• Half step (H) = Next key (e.g., E → F, no key between)

1. Understanding Roman Numerals

Roman numerals represent chords built on different scale degrees. Here's what they mean:

Roman Numeral Chord Quality Example in C Major
I, IV, V Major chords (uppercase) C, F, G
ii, iii, vi Minor chords (lowercase) Dm, Em, Am
vii° Diminished chord Bdim
💡 Quick Tip: Uppercase = bright/happy major chord. Lowercase = darker/sadder minor chord.

2. The Chromatic Scale (All 12 Notes)

These are all the notes available on a keyboard, repeating every octave:

C C# D D# E F F# G G# A A# B

White boxes = white keys. Black boxes = black keys.

Note: C# and Db are the same key (enharmonic equivalents).

3. Using the W-W-H-W-W-W-H Pattern

Now let's use THE PATTERN to build a major scale from any root note:

W - W - H - W - W - W - H

Start on any note and follow this pattern to get your major scale!

Example: C Major Scale

Starting from C and applying W-W-H-W-W-W-H:

  1. Start: C
  2. W (Whole): C → D (skip C#)
  3. W (Whole): D → E (skip D#)
  4. H (Half): E → F (no key to skip)
  5. W (Whole): F → G (skip F#)
  6. W (Whole): G → A (skip G#)
  7. W (Whole): A → B (skip A#)
  8. H (Half): B → C (no key to skip)
C Major Scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C
✓ All white keys! Perfect for beginners!

Example: G Major Scale

Starting from G and applying the SAME pattern W-W-H-W-W-W-H:

  1. Start: G
  2. W: G → A
  3. W: A → B
  4. H: B → C
  5. W: C → D
  6. W: D → E
  7. W: E → F# (need the black key to maintain the pattern!)
  8. H: F# → G
G Major Scale: G - A - B - C - D - E - F# - G
Note: F# is needed because the pattern requires a whole step from E
💡 Master This: The W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern works from ANY starting note. Try it from D, F, A, or any key on the keyboard!

4. Building Natural Chords from Your Scale

Here's the key insight: For chords that are natural to the major scale (like I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii°), you use the SAME simple method - just stack every other note from your scale!

The Skip-Pick Formula:

Pick a note → Skip one → Pick a note → Skip one → Pick a note

The chord quality (major or minor) happens automatically based on which scale degree you start from!

💡 Note: This skip-pick method works for all the natural chords in the key. If you want a chord that's NOT natural to the scale (like VI major instead of vi minor), those are called "borrowed chords" - see Section 8 for details. The Calculate Chords tool handles them automatically!

Building Major Chords (I, IV, V)

Example: C Major (I)
C major scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B

Start on C (1st):
1. C (start)
2. Skip D
3. E (pick)
4. Skip F
5. G (pick)
→ C + E + G
Example: F Major (IV)
C major scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B

Start on F (4th):
1. F (start)
2. Skip G
3. A (pick)
4. Skip B
5. C (pick - wrap around to start of scale)
→ F + A + C

Building Minor Chords (ii, iii, vi) - Same Method!

You do exactly the same thing! Start on a different scale degree and skip-pick-skip-pick.

Example: Am (vi)
C major scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B

Start on A (6th):
1. A (start)
2. Skip B
3. C (pick - wrap around)
4. Skip D
5. E (pick)
→ A + C + E

This automatically makes a minor chord because of the spacing between A-C-E!
Example: Dm (ii)
C major scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B

Start on D (2nd):
1. D (start)
2. Skip E
3. F (pick)
4. Skip G
5. A (pick)
→ D + F + A

This is automatically minor!
💡 The Secret: You don't need different formulas for major vs minor! Just:
1. Use your major scale
2. Start on the right note
3. Skip-pick-skip-pick
The chord quality happens naturally!

5. Chord Inversions (Umkehrungen)

Once you know how to build chords, you can rearrange their notes to create different voicings. This is called a chord inversion (German: Tonumkehrung or Akkordumkehrung).

💡 The Big Idea

A chord inversion is when you take the bottom note and move it to the top (or vice versa). The chord is still the same, but it sounds different!

The Three Positions

Every triad (3-note chord) has three possible positions:

Example: C Major Chord (C + E + G)

Root Position: C - E - G
The root note (C) is on the bottom. This is the "normal" way.

First Inversion: E - G - C
Move the bottom note (C) to the top. Now E is the lowest note.

Second Inversion: G - C - E
Move the bottom note (E) to the top again. Now G is the lowest note.
Example: Am Chord (A + C + E)

Root Position: A - C - E
First Inversion: C - E - A
Second Inversion: E - A - C

Understanding Chord Note Names

Each note in a chord has a specific name based on its interval from the root:

Chord Note Terminology:

• Root (German: Grundton) - The first note, gives the chord its name
• Third (German: Terz) - The second note, determines if major or minor
• Fifth (German: Quinte) - The third note
• Bass Note (German: Basston) - The lowest note being played
Example: C Major Chord

Root Position (C - E - G):
• Root: C
• Third: E
• Fifth: G
• Bass note: C (the root is on bottom)

First Inversion (E - G - C):
• Root: C
• Third: E
• Fifth: G
• Bass note: E (the third is on bottom)

Second Inversion (G - C - E):
• Root: C
• Third: E
• Fifth: G
• Bass note: G (the fifth is on bottom)

Notice: The root, third, and fifth never change - only the bass note changes!
💡 Key Insight: The root, third, and fifth are always the same notes regardless of inversion. What changes is which note is the bass note (lowest note). This is what creates the different inversion sounds!

Why Use Inversions?

Inversions make your playing sound more professional and musical:

  1. Smoother Movement: Instead of jumping your hand all over the keyboard, you can stay in one position and just shift fingers slightly
  2. Better Bass Lines: Create more interesting bass movement by choosing which note is on the bottom
  3. Richer Sound: Different inversions have different emotional qualities - experiment to find what sounds best
  4. Voice Leading: Move between chords more smoothly by keeping common notes in place
Practical Example: I - IV - V in C Major

Without Inversions (all root position):
C (C-E-G) → F (F-A-C) → G (G-B-D)
Your hand jumps around a lot!

With Inversions (smooth voice leading):
C (C-E-G) → F (C-F-A) → G (B-D-G)
Much smoother! Notice how C stays in place, and your hand barely moves.

How to Identify Inversions

Look at which note is the bass note (lowest note):

Bass Note (Basston) Inversion Example (C chord)
Root Root Position C - E - G
Third (Terz) First Inversion E - G - C
Fifth (Quinte) Second Inversion G - C - E
🎹 Pro Tip: When playing chord progressions, try using different inversions instead of always playing root position. Your playing will sound more musical and less "choppy"! Experiment to find what sounds best to your ear.
💡 Quick Practice: Pick any chord you know (like C, G, or Am) and practice playing all three positions without stopping. This builds finger flexibility and helps you "see" the chord shapes on the keyboard.

7. Understanding Natural Chords (Diatonic Chords)

Natural chords (also called "diatonic chords") are chords that are built using only the notes from your scale. When you use the skip-pick-skip-pick method, you're building natural chords!

The Natural Chord Pattern in Major Keys

In ANY major key, when you build chords using skip-pick from the scale, you always get the same pattern of chord qualities:

Degree Roman Numeral Quality Example in C Major
1stIMajorC (C-E-G)
2ndiiMinorDm (D-F-A)
3rdiiiMinorEm (E-G-B)
4thIVMajorF (F-A-C)
5thVMajorG (G-B-D)
6thviMinorAm (A-C-E)
7thvii°DiminishedBdim (B-D-F)
💡 The Pattern Never Changes!
Major - minor - minor - Major - Major - minor - diminished
I - ii - iii - IV - V - vi - vii°

This pattern is the same in EVERY major key. C major, G major, F# major - they all follow this exact pattern!

How to Tell If a Chord Is Natural

A chord is natural if it matches the expected quality at that position in the scale. Here's how to check:

Example 1: I-V-vi-IV (Natural Progression)

Looking at the pattern above:
• I at position 1 = should be Major ✓ Matches!
• V at position 5 = should be Major ✓ Matches!
• vi at position 6 = should be minor ✓ Matches!
• IV at position 4 = should be Major ✓ Matches!

All chords use only notes from the scale = 100% natural progression!
Example 2: I-IV-♭VII-IV (Contains Borrowed Chord)

Looking at the pattern above:
• I at position 1 = should be Major ✓ Natural
• IV at position 4 = should be Major ✓ Natural
• ♭VII at position 7 = should be diminished (vii°), but we want Major ✗ BORROWED!
• IV at position 4 = should be Major ✓ Natural

The ♭VII chord uses notes outside the scale = Borrowed chord!

Why Do We Use Borrowed Chords?

Natural chords sound smooth and familiar because all notes come from one scale. But sometimes you want:

  • More drama: Borrowed chords add unexpected color and emotion
  • Different mood: Natural vi is minor and sad, but borrowed VI is major and powerful
  • Variety: Using only natural chords can sound predictable
  • Genre style: Rock loves ♭VII, classical loves iv (minor four), R&B loves ♭III
💡 Quick Check:
• If the Roman numeral matches the table above = Natural chord (use skip-pick)
• If the Roman numeral doesn't match = Borrowed chord (uses notes outside the scale)

The Calculate Chords tool automatically detects this and highlights borrowed chords in orange!

8. What Are Borrowed Chords?

Sometimes you'll see a chord that doesn't match the natural pattern above - like VI major instead of vi minor. These are called borrowed chords!

Why "Borrowed"?

These chords are "borrowed" from the parallel minor (or major) key. They add color and emotion to progressions by breaking the expected pattern.

Common Examples:

♭VII (flat-seven major) - Instead of vii° diminished
Example: In C major, B♭ major instead of B diminished
Sound: Powerful, rock-influenced

♭VI (flat-six major) - Instead of vi minor
Example: In C major, A♭ major instead of A minor
Sound: Dark, dramatic

iv (four minor) - Instead of IV major
Example: In C major, F minor instead of F major
Sound: Sad, melancholic

♭III (flat-three major) - Instead of iii minor
Example: In C major, E♭ major instead of E minor
Sound: Uplifting but unexpected

How to Play Them

The skip-pick-skip-pick method from your scale WON'T work for borrowed chords - they use notes outside your scale!

💡 Easy Solution: Use the Calculate Chords tool! It automatically calculates the correct notes for borrowed chords using interval formulas. Just enter your progression and it shows you exactly what to play.

Interval Formulas (For Reference)

If you're curious, here's how borrowed chords are built:

Major chord: Root + 4 semitones + 3 semitones
Minor chord: Root + 3 semitones + 4 semitones
Diminished chord: Root + 3 semitones + 3 semitones

9. Minor Scales - The Other Essential Pattern

Just like major scales have a specific pattern, minor scales have their own pattern that creates a darker, more emotional sound.

W - H - W - W - H - W - W
Whole - Half - Whole - Whole - Half - Whole - Whole
💡 Compare the Patterns:
Major: W - W - H - W - W - W - H (bright, happy)
Minor: W - H - W - W - H - W - W (dark, sad)

Building a Natural Minor Scale

The process is exactly the same as major - just use a different pattern!

Example: A Natural Minor Scale

Starting from A and applying W-H-W-W-H-W-W:

  1. Start: A
  2. W (Whole): A → B (skip A#)
  3. H (Half): B → C (no key to skip)
  4. W (Whole): C → D (skip C#)
  5. W (Whole): D → E (skip D#)
  6. H (Half): E → F (no key to skip)
  7. W (Whole): F → G (skip F#)
  8. W (Whole): G → A (skip G#)
A Natural Minor Scale: A - B - C - D - E - F - G - A
✓ All white keys! Perfect for beginners learning minor!

Relative Major and Minor

Here's something cool: every major scale has a "relative minor" that uses the exact same notes, just starting from a different position!

C Major and A Minor - Same Notes!

C Major scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C
A Minor scale: A - B - C - D - E - F - G - A

Same 7 notes (all white keys), different starting point!
C major sounds bright, A minor sounds dark.

When Do I Use Minor?

If you see a progression starting with lowercase "i" (like i - VI - III - VII), you're in a minor key. Use the W-H-W-W-H-W-W pattern!
