Roman Numeral Notation

Understanding I, IV, V, vi and chord relationships

Understanding Roman Numerals

Roman numerals represent chords built on different scale degrees. Here's what they mean:

Roman Numeral Chord Quality Example in C Major
I, IV, V Major chords (uppercase) C, F, G
ii, iii, vi Minor chords (lowercase) Dm, Em, Am
vii° Diminished chord Bdim
💡 Quick Tip: Uppercase = bright/happy major chord. Lowercase = darker/sadder minor chord.

Why Use Roman Numerals?

Roman numerals are incredibly useful because they show the relationship between chords, not just the specific notes:

  • Key-Independent: I-V-vi-IV works in ANY key - just apply it to different root notes
  • Shows Function: The numeral tells you the chord's role (I = home, V = tension, vi = emotional)
  • Easy to Transpose: Learn one progression in Roman numerals, play it in all 12 keys
  • Universal Language: Musicians worldwide use this system to communicate chord progressions

Common Progressions

I - V - vi - IV (Pop Progression)
• In C: C - G - Am - F
• In G: G - D - Em - C
• In D: D - A - Bm - G

Same pattern, different keys!
ii - V - I (Jazz Progression)
• In C: Dm - G - C
• In G: Am - D - G
• In F: Gm - C - F

This progression creates a strong resolution to the I chord.

The Seven Scale Degrees

Each Roman numeral corresponds to a position in the major scale:

In C Major Scale (C-D-E-F-G-A-B):

• I = 1st note (C) = C major
• ii = 2nd note (D) = D minor
• iii = 3rd note (E) = E minor
• IV = 4th note (F) = F major
• V = 5th note (G) = G major
• vi = 6th note (A) = A minor
• vii° = 7th note (B) = B diminished

Borrowed Chords (Advanced)

Sometimes you'll see Roman numerals that don't match the standard pattern, like:

  • ♭VII - Flat-seven major (e.g., B♭ in C major instead of Bdim)
  • ♭VI - Flat-six major (e.g., A♭ in C major instead of Am)
  • iv - Four minor (e.g., Fm in C major instead of F major)

These are called borrowed chords - they add color and emotion by breaking the expected pattern.

🎹 Practice Tip: Pick a simple progression like I-IV-V-I and practice it in different keys using the Calculate Chords tool. You'll see how the Roman numerals stay the same while the actual notes change!
